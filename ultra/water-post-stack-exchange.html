    What is the amount of drinkable water in Earth? Always same, less, more? [on hold]

    up vote 1 down vote favorite
    2

    Problem

    Is the distribution availability of drinkable water an actual problem?

    I'm not in the field directly but I understand there should be certain amount of matters of fact that could determine available and usable water along history.

    THE MAIN QUESTION

    The topic is out there: How can our blue planet be running out of fresh water?. Another independent journalism shows the same path: The World Is Running Out of Water. I'm interested in not the actual history of water on Earth but rather on these questions posed for earthscience.SE:

    How much (measureable) potable(/utilizable) water is available on Earth?

    Is there a traceable history of human uses of water on Earth?

    What credit do specialists give to this crisis of the anthropocene?

    May human's impact on the planet have changed the amount of this resource? I'd assume some geologists, and many other specialists, could argue in favor or against, but I'd figure this is answerable measuring different types of facts.

    (I know there can be different schools of thought, and/or different ways to measure the amount of available resources, but I'd like to see some way of measuring the problem. Not all is a mere matter of opinion I'd add.)

    Speculation

    maybe off-topic, but can add to the understanding of the central problem

    I'd assume that in the past centuries a lot has advanced: technology has made possible to get large amounts of drinkable water. But in the same coin, I'd say that some has got worse, and some other can get better. Are there a range of reasonable expectations?

    Do 1 billion people live without water? That's what the charity water NGO states. If so, is it affecting education, health, poverty and others? How are we producing such a bad situation?

    May it get worse? Michael Burry, Real-Life Market Genius From The Big Short suggests this in said movie. In a recent interview, at nymag.com, he pointed out that he started looking at investments in water about 15 years ago. "Fresh, clean water cannot be taken for granted". And that transporting water is impractical for both political and physical reasons:

    food is the way to invest in water. That is, grow food in water-rich areas and transport it for sale in water-poor areas. This is the method for redistributing water that is least contentious, and ultimately it can be profitable, which will ensure that this redistribution is sustainable. A bottle of wine takes over 400 bottles of water to produce â€” the water embedded in food is what I found interesting.

    So if this guy anticipated the market fall a couple of years ago, should we be worried about what he's saying now about water?
